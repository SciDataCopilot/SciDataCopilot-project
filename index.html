SciDataCopilot Toward Scientific AI-Ready Data Preparation

Scientific Data Team AT AI4S Center, Shanghai Artificial Intelligence
Paper Code arXiv

Figure 1. We define the Scientific AI-Ready data paradigm as a paradigm for scientific data preparation. Abstract

Recent progress in AI for Science has been driven primarily by AI-ready scientific corpora derived from structured literature. However, extending these approaches to experimental scientific data remains challenging due to task-dependent data requirements, fragmented data sources across disciplines, and tight coupling between data processing and domain-specific expertise. To address this, we propose SciDataCopilot, a unified multi-agent framework grounded in a novel Scientific AI-Ready Scientific Data paradigm. Scientific AI-Ready extends conventional AI-ready data by explicitly formalizing how scientific data are specified, structured, and composed with respect to downstream computational models and experimental workflows. Specifically, the paradigm introduces task-conditioned data specification to map scientific objectives to data schemas and constraints. It further defines model-aware data structuring and principled cross-domain data composition, enabling heterogeneous experimental data to be transformed into interoperable, model-consumable forms. SciDataCopilot instantiates this paradigm through a unified workflow that performs data ingestion, scientific intent parsing, data processing, and multimodal data integration. By placing data at the core of scientific workflows, SciDataCopilot provides a principled foundation for reusable, transferable systems, enabling the transition toward general and experiment-driven scientific intelligence.

Framework

Architecture of SciDataCopilot. The framework integrates four collaborative agents (Data Access, Intent Parsing, Data Processing, and Data Integration) to autonomously align user intents with complex data resources. Ultimately, SciDataCopilot bridges heterogeneous scientific data with specific models, redefining task-guided data customization and cross-disciplinary integration to empower diverse scientific research tasks.

INSTRUCTION

Using the MNE fieldtrip_cmc MEG dataset, perform Spatio-Spectral Decomposition (SSD) by resampling the raw data to 250 Hz and cropping it to a 60-second window (50–110s). Fit the SSD estimator using OAS regularization and zero-phase FIR filters, defining the signal band at 9–12 Hz and the noise band at 8–13 Hz (both with 1 Hz transition bandwidths) to isolate alpha oscillations from 1/f background activity. Validate the results by inverting the spatial filters to plot patterns, transforming the data to source space, and comparing the Welch PSD of the maximum and minimum SNR components to verify oscillatory enhancement.

Plan
  • Step 1: Acquire the MNE fieldtrip_cmc raw MEG dataset; mark bad channels and select only MEG channels, restricting analysis to planar gradiometers to avoid sensor-type scaling biases.
  • Step 2: Resample the continuous raw data to 250 Hz using antialiasing consistent with MEG preprocessing.
  • Step 3: Optionally perform artifact mitigation on the resampled continuous data (e.g., SSP/ICA for EOG/ECG and SSS/Maxwell filtering if available), and apply a 50/60 Hz notch to suppress line noise.
  • Step 4: Define zero-phase FIR filter designs with 1 Hz transition bandwidths and padding long enough to avoid edge artifacts at 250 Hz; ensure feasibility of the filter lengths (automatic length selection) and zero-phase application.
  • Step 5: Specify the SSD signal band as 9–12 Hz (zero-phase FIR, 1 Hz transitions).
  • Step 6: Specify flanking SSD noise bands as two separate zero-phase FIR bandpasses: 8–9 Hz and 12–13 Hz (each with 1 Hz transitions). Do not include the signal band inside a single wide noise band.
  • Step 7: Apply the three zero-phase FIR bandpasses (signal and two flanks) to the continuous resampled gradiometer data before any cropping, using adequate padding to avoid filter edge artifacts.
  • Step 8: Crop the three filtered datasets to the 60 s analysis window (50–110 s), ensuring identical time spans and sensor selections.
  • Step 9: Compute the signal-band sensor covariance from the 9–12 Hz filtered, cropped data.
  • Step 10: Compute the two flank-band sensor covariances from the 8–9 Hz and 12–13 Hz filtered, cropped data, then form the SSD noise covariance as the sum or average of the two flank covariances.
  • Step 11: Apply OAS regularization to both the signal-band and noise-band covariances.
  • Step 12: Solve the generalized eigenvalue problem that maximizes the ratio of signal-band to noise-band power to obtain SSD spatial filters (W) and eigenvalues (SNR scores), and sort components by descending eigenvalue.
  • Step 13: Prepare broadband projection data by selecting the same sensors and cropping interval (50–110 s) from the resampled data; optionally apply a zero-phase 1–45 Hz bandpass to the continuous data before cropping (or use unfiltered broadband) to avoid trivial band-shaping during validation.
  • Step 14: Compute the broadband data covariance (C_data) from the broadband cropped data of Step 13 to be used for interpretable pattern estimation.
  • Step 15: Compute spatial patterns (sensor topographies) as A = C_data × W, and visualize the top-ranked patterns to assess plausible alpha generator topographies.
  • Step 16: Project the broadband cropped data (from Step 13) through the SSD spatial filters (W) to obtain SSD component (source-space) time series.
  • Step 17: Compute Welch power spectral densities for all SSD component time series using parameters that yield frequency resolution ≤ 0.5 Hz (e.g., segment length ≥ 4 s with 50% overlap, Hann window, detrend set to constant), and evaluate spectra across a broad range (e.g., 1–45 Hz).
  • Step 18: Identify the components with maximum and minimum SSD SNR (largest and smallest eigenvalues) and compare their Welch PSDs to verify oscillatory enhancement: confirm a pronounced alpha peak (9–12 Hz) in the maximum-SNR component and attenuation in the minimum-SNR component.
  • Step 19: Quantify alpha-band SNR by comparing integrated power in 9–12 Hz against the immediate flank bands (8–9 Hz and 12–13 Hz), and report ratios for the maximum- and minimum-SNR components.
  • Step 20: Document and save SSD outputs, including spatial filters (W), spatial patterns (A), component time series, eigenvalues, and PSD comparisons, together with notes on filter parameters, padding strategy, sensor selection, and any artifact cleaning applied.
Analysis
  • Summary of what the code does
  • - Loads the MNE fieldtrip_cmc MEG dataset and keeps the CTF axial-gradiometer sensor set (MNE denotes CTF axial gradiometers as “mag”, which is what your picker selects).
  • - Resamples to 250 Hz with MNE’s built-in anti-alias FIR, applies zero-phase FIR notch at 50/100 Hz, and band-pass filters:
  • - Signal band: 9–12 Hz (alpha)
  • - Noise (flank) bands: 8–9 Hz and 12–13 Hz
  • - All filters are zero-phase FIR (firwin) with 1 Hz transition bandwidths.
  • - Crops to 50–110 s to get a contiguous 60-s segment.
  • - Computes OAS-regularized covariances from the band-passed data:
  • - Cs from the 9–12 Hz band
  • - Cn from the concatenation of the two flank bands (8–9 and 12–13 Hz)
  • - Solves the generalized eigenvalue problem Cs w = λ Cn w to obtain spatial filters W (SSD filters) and eigenvalues λ (SSD SNRs).
  • - Computes spatial patterns by inverting the filters using the broadband (1–45 Hz) covariance: A = C_data W, and visualizes the first six patterns as sensor topomaps (averaging CTF triplets with identical positions to avoid plotting duplicates).
  • - Projects broadband data into SSD source space S = W^T X_bb, computes Welch PSD for each component, and compares the component with the maximum SNR eigenvalue to the one with the minimum SNR eigenvalue, both visually (PSD plot) and via an alpha SNR ratio (alpha power / average flank power).
  • - Saves all results, figures, and a JSON report with key metrics.
  • Scientific interpretation
  • - What the SSD is optimizing: The generalized eigenvectors w maximize the ratio of 9–12 Hz power to the power in the adjacent 8–9 and 12–13 Hz bands.
  • - Expected spectral outcome:
  • - Maximum-SNR component: A clear, narrow peak near 10–11 Hz in the PSD.
  • - Minimum-SNR component: No pronounced alpha peak.
  • - Expected spatial outcome (patterns):
  • - The spatial patterns A are appropriate for neurophysiological interpretation.
  • - Validation logic:
  • - Rank-order consistency.
  • - Alpha enhancement.
  • - Spatial plausibility.
  • Methodological notes and minor code review
  • - Channel picking: pick_meg_grad selects meg='mag'.
  • - Covariance estimation: OAS is appropriate.
  • - Noise covariance and flank concatenation: good practice.
  • - Generalized eigen-solver: whitening via Cholesky.
  • - Patterns scaling: normalization is acceptable.
  • - Zero-phase filtering: avoids temporal shifts.
  • - Deduplication of CTF triplets.
  • - PSD parameters are reasonable.
  • What to look for in your outputs
  • - reports/ssd_analysis_report.json
  • - figures/ssd_psd_max_min.png
  • - figures/ssd_top_patterns.png
  • Potential improvements or sensitivity checks
  • - Broader signal bands.
  • - Shift time window.
  • - Improved pattern scaling.
  • - Adjust for beta-band CMC.
  • Bottom line
  • - The implementation meets the requirement.
  • - Successful SSD characteristics.
  • - Use reports and figures for confirmation.
